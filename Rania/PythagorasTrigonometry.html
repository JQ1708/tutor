Pythagoras' Theorem

Who was Pythagoras?

(Hover over me to find out!)

He was a Greek mathematician who lived over 2500 years ago. He's most famous for his theorem about right-angled triangles!

What is Pythagoras' Theorem?

It's a formula linking the lengths of the three sides of a right-angled triangle.

The longest side, opposite the right angle, is called the hypotenuse.

Remember to refer to the diagram of a right-angled triangle with sides labelled 'a', 'b', and 'c' (hypotenuse).

a¬≤ + b¬≤ = c¬≤

How to Find the Hypotenuse (c)

  • Step 1: Square the two shorter sides (a¬≤ and b¬≤).
  • Step 2: Add them together.
  • Step 3: Take the positive square root.
c = √a¬≤ + b¬≤

How to Find a Shorter Side (a or b)

  • Step 1: Square the hypotenuse (c¬≤) and the other short side (b¬≤).
  • Step 2: Subtract the smaller from the larger.
  • Step 3: Take the positive square root.
a = √c¬≤ - b¬≤

Problem Solving with Pythagoras

You can use the theorem on any shape where you can create a right-angled triangle!

  • Splitting an isosceles triangle down the middle.
  • Finding the diagonal of a rectangle or square.
  • In 3D shapes like pyramids and cuboids.

Quick Check!

In a right-angled triangle, side a = 6 and side b = 8. What is the length of the hypotenuse c?

Answer: c¬≤ = 6¬≤ + 8¬≤ = 36 + 64 = 100. So, c = √100 = 10.

What is Trigonometry?

Trigonometry is the relationship between the sides and angles in a right-angled triangle. We use it to find missing sides or angles.

The 3 Sides

Please refer to the diagram showing the positions of these sides relative to an angle θ.

  • Hypotenuse: Always the longest side, opposite the right angle.
  • Opposite: The side opposite the angle you are working with (θ).
  • Adjacent: The side next to the angle you are working with (θ).

The Magic Word: SOHCAHTOA

This mnemonic helps you remember the three trig ratios:

Sin θ = Opposite / Hypotenuse
Cos θ = Adjacent / Hypotenuse
Tan θ = Opposite / Adjacent

How to Find a Missing Side

  • Label the sides of the triangle (H, O, A) relative to the given angle.
  • Decide which ratio to use: SOH, CAH, or TOA.
  • Substitute the known values into the formula.
  • Solve for the unknown side.

  • Label the sides of the triangle (H, O, A).

Angles of Elevation & Depression

What are they?

These are special angles used in real-world problems (like finding the height of a building).

Angle of Elevation

The angle measured UPWARDS from the horizontal. Think of elevating your gaze.

Angle of Depression

The angle measured DOWNWARDS from the horizontal. Think of being depressed and looking down.

Super Important Facts!

  • Both angles are ALWAYS measured from the HORIZONTAL line.
  • Because the horizontal lines are parallel, the angle of elevation is EQUAL to the angle of depression. They are alternate angles (a 'Z' angle).
  • Always draw a diagram for these problems!
  • Remember to refer to the diagrams which show a person looking up at a tree (elevation) and a person on a cliff looking down at a boat (depression).

Exact Trig Values

Why do we need them?

Sometimes you need to give an answer in its exact form (using fractions and square roots) instead of a long decimal. This is common in non-calculator exams.

The Two Magic Triangles

We can find these exact values from two special triangles. Make sure to refer to the diagrams for these!

  • An isosceles right-angled triangle with shorter sides of length 1. This gives values for 45¬∞.
  • An equilateral triangle of side length 2, cut in half. This gives values for 30¬∞ and 60¬∞.

Trig Graphs

You might be asked for a coordinate on a trig graph.

Example: The coordinate (30, k) is on the graph y = tan x. Find k.
