Pythagoras in 3D

The Core Idea

The secret to 3D problems is finding the 2D right-angled triangles hidden inside the 3D shapes!

For example, in a cone, the perpendicular height, the base radius, and the slanted height form a right-angled triangle.

Please refer to the diagram of the cone to see this triangle.

The Special 3D Formula

For finding the space diagonal of a cuboid (a line connecting opposite corners), there's a special version of Pythagoras' theorem.

Refer to the diagram of a cuboid with dimensions x, y, z and space diagonal d.

d¬≤ = x¬≤ + y¬≤ + z¬≤

How do you get that formula?

It's just using Pythagoras' theorem twice!

  • First: Find the diagonal across the base of the cuboid. Let's call it 'b'. You'll have b¬≤ = x¬≤ + y¬≤.
  • Second: Now, imagine a new triangle inside the cuboid, made of the base diagonal (b), the height (z), and the space diagonal (d).
  • Using Pythagoras again: d¬≤ = b¬≤ + z¬≤.
  • Substitute the first formula into the second: d¬≤ = (x¬≤ + y¬≤) + z¬≤.

Trigonometry in 3D

How do I use SOHCAHTOA in 3D?

It's exactly the same principle as in 2D! The challenge is correctly identifying the 2D triangle you need to work with.

Examiner's Top Tip

Once you've identified the right triangle in the 3D shape, draw it separately as a 2D triangle. This makes it much easier to see the Opposite, Adjacent, and Hypotenuse sides.

Steps to Success

  • Read the question carefully to understand which angle or length you need to find.
  • Find a right-angled triangle within the 3D shape that contains that angle or length.
  • You might need to use Pythagoras first to find a missing side in your chosen triangle.
  • Draw your 2D triangle and label the known sides and angles.
  • Apply SOHCAHTOA as normal.

Common Shapes

Be ready to find triangles in:

  • Cuboids
  • Pyramids (both square-based and triangular-based)
  • Cones
  • Triangular Prisms

Always check the diagrams provided in the question carefully!

Angle Between a Line and a Plane

Finding the Angle

This sounds complicated, but there's a simple process. It's the angle between the line and its projection onto the plane.

Imagine the plane is the floor. The projection is the shadow the line would cast if a light was directly above it.

Please refer to the diagram showing a line, its projection (shadow) on a plane, and the right-angled triangle they form with the 'height' from the line to the plane.

  • You will always form a right-angled triangle.
  • The angle is found using SOHCAHTOA within this triangle.
