What is a Solution?

The magic of dissolving explained with particles.

When you mix sugar in water, it seems to disappear. This is **dissolving**. The result is a **solution**.

  • The substance that dissolves is the **solute** (e.g., sugar).
  • The liquid it dissolves in is the **solvent** (e.g., water).
  • In a solution, the solute particles spread out evenly among the solvent particles. They are too small to see, which is why solutions are **transparent**.

Mass is Conserved

Dissolving isn't a disappearing act. Nothing is lost!

10g Solute + 100g Solvent = 110g Solution

The total mass stays the same.

When a solute dissolves, its particles are still present in the solution, just spread out. Because no particles are lost, no mass is lost.

The total mass of the solution is simply the mass of the solute plus the mass of the solvent.

Key Principle: Mass is always conserved in physical changes like dissolving.

Concentration

How much "stuff" is in the solution?

Concentration describes the amount of solute dissolved in a certain amount of solvent.

  • A **dilute** solution has a small amount of solute.
  • A **concentrated** solution has a large amount of solute.
  • A **saturated** solution contains the maximum amount of solute that can be dissolved at that temperature. If you add more, it won't dissolve.

Paper Chromatography

Separating mixtures based on solubility.

Chromatography is a technique used to separate the components of a mixture. For inks, it works because different coloured dyes have different solubilities.

  • The paper is placed in a solvent (like water or ethanol).
  • As the solvent travels up the paper, it dissolves the ink spot and carries the dyes with it.
  • **More soluble** dyes travel **further** up the paper.
  • **Less soluble** dyes travel a **shorter** distance.
