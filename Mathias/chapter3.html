Variation Within a Species

  • Continuous variation: Can be any value within a range (e.g., height, skin color). Shown on a frequency diagram without gaps.
  • Discontinuous variation: Has distinct options (e.g., rolling tongues, blood group). Shown on a bar chart with gaps.
  • Causes: Inherited variation (from parents) and Environmental variation (from surroundings, e.g., wind shaping a tree). Some traits are both.

Chromosomes, Genes & DNA

  • Every cell nucleus contains chromosomes. Humans have 46 chromosomes (23 pairs).
  • Chromosomes are made of a long DNA molecule folded with proteins.
  • DNA forms a double-helix shape. Discovered by Watson & Crick (using Rosalind Franklin's X-ray photos).
  • Genes are sections of DNA that contain instructions for a characteristic (e.g., eye color).

Fertilisation & Sex Determination

  • Gametes (sex cells) have half the chromosomes (23 in humans). Male: Sperm cell, Female: Egg cell.
  • Fertilisation occurs when sperm and egg nuclei fuse, making a fertilised egg cell (46 chromosomes).
  • Sex Determination: Females have XX sex chromosomes. Males have XY.
  • An egg always has an X. A sperm has either an X or Y. If an X-sperm fertilises the egg = female. If a Y-sperm fertilises = male.

Fetal Growth & Development

  • The fertilised egg becomes an embryo, then a fetus after organs and heartbeat form for the 40-week gestation.
  • Diet & Health: Pregnant women need folic acid (prevents spina bifida) and balanced nutrients.
  • Avoid: Certain foods with bacteria (e.g., blue cheese), drugs, and tobacco smoke.
  • Carbon monoxide and Nicotine in smoke reduce oxygen for the fetus, causing low birth weight or stillbirth.

Natural Selection & Evolution

  • Adaptations are characteristics that help organisms survive in an ecosystem (e.g., polar bear fur).
  • Because of genetic variation, some individuals have slightly better adaptations.
  • They survive longer and reproduce, passing these genes on. This process is Natural Selection.
  • Over millions of years, this causes gradual changes in a species, known as Evolution (theory by Charles Darwin).
