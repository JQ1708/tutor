Chemical Bonds

  • Particles composed of two or more atoms joined together form molecules.
  • Covalent Bonds: Formed when two non-metal atoms share a pair of electrons in their outer shell. We use dot-and-cross diagrams to model them.
  • Ions: Charged particles. Metal atoms lose electrons to become positive ions. Non-metals gain electrons to become negative ions.
  • Ionic Bonds: Formed when metals react with non-metals by transferring electrons. Strong electrostatic attraction holds the positive and negative ions together.

Simple & Giant Structures

  • Giant Structures: Made of atoms or ions with very strong bonds between every particle in a continuous network.
    • Examples: Sodium chloride (ionic), Diamond (giant covalent), Metals.
    • Properties: Very high melting and boiling points (needs lots of energy to break bonds). Metals conduct electricity due to free-moving electrons.
  • Simple Structures: Made of molecules (e.g., water, carbon dioxide).
    • Properties: Strong covalent bonds hold atoms inside the molecule, but only weak attractive forces exist between the molecules. Low melting points; often liquids or gases at room temperature.

Density

  • Density measures how much mass is packed into a certain volume.
  • Formula: Density = Mass ÷ Volume (e.g., g/cm³).
  • Measuring Volume: For a regular solid, multiply width × length × height. For an irregular solid, immerse it in a measuring cylinder with water and measure how much the water level rises (water displacement).
  • States of Matter: Solids have highest density (particles tightly packed). Liquids are slightly lower. Gases have very low density (particles spread far apart).
