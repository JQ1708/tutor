Conservation of Mass & Energy

  • Law of conservation of mass: In a chemical reaction, no atoms are lost or gained. Mass of reactants = Mass of products.
  • When charcoal burns, its mass decreases because carbon dioxide gas escapes into the air, but the total mass of the atoms remains the same.
  • When metals are heated in air, mass increases because oxygen from the air joins the metal to form an oxide.
  • Law of conservation of energy: Energy cannot be created or destroyed; it is transferred from the chemical bonds of the reactants to the products or to the surroundings.

Making & Purifying Salts

  • Metals + Acids: Produce salt + hydrogen gas. (e.g., zinc + sulfuric acid → zinc sulfate + hydrogen)
  • Metal Carbonates + Acids: Produce salt + water + carbon dioxide gas.
  • Purification Process:
    1. React metal/carbonate with acid until there is unreacted solid left (excess).
    2. Filtration: Use filter paper and funnel to remove the unreacted solid.
    3. Evaporation & Crystallisation: Heat the salt solution gently to evaporate water, then let it cool slowly to form large crystals.

Displacement Reactions

  • A displacement reaction occurs when a more reactive element takes the place of a less reactive element in a compound.
  • Example: Iron + Copper sulfate → Iron sulfate + Copper. (Because Iron is higher in the Reactivity Series than Copper).
  • Example: Copper + Magnesium sulfate → No Reaction. (Copper is less reactive than Magnesium).

Rates of Reaction

  • Rate is the speed of a reaction. Measured by calculating how fast gas escapes (measuring mass) or how fast gas is collected (using a gas syringe/cylinder).
  • Reactions happen when particles collide with enough energy. Rate increases when you increase:
  • Temperature: Particles move faster, collide more often, and with more energy.
  • Concentration: More particles in the same space = more frequent collisions.
  • Surface Area: Breaking a solid into powder exposes more particles to react, leading to more frequent collisions.

Law of Conservation of Mass
In a chemical reaction, mass is neither created nor destroyed. The mass of reactants equals the mass of products.
Displacement Reaction
A reaction where a more reactive element takes the place of a less reactive element in a compound.
Filtration
A separation technique used to remove an insoluble solid (like excess unreacted metal) from a solution.
Crystallisation
A separation technique used to isolate a soluble solid (salt) from a liquid by evaporating the solvent.
Reactivity Series
A list of metals ordered by their reactivity with the most reactive metals at the top.
Rate of Reaction
The speed at which a chemical reaction happens. It is increased by higher temperature, concentration, or surface area.
Collision Theory
For a chemical reaction to occur, the particles of the reactants must collide with each other with enough energy.
