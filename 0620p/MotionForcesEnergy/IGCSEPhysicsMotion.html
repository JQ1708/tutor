1. Speed & Velocity

Definitions

Speed is the distance travelled per unit time. It is a scalar quantity (magnitude only).

Velocity is the speed in a given direction. It is a vector quantity (magnitude and direction).

Example: A car on a circular track has a constant speed but a changing velocity because its direction is always changing!

4. Freefall

An object is in freefall when the only force acting on it is gravity. On Earth, the acceleration of freefall (g) is ‚âà 10 m/s2.

However, falling on Earth involves air resistance...

The Story of a Skydiver: Reaching Terminal Velocity

1

The Jump

Initially, the only significant force is weight. Air resistance is negligible. The skydiver accelerates downwards at ‚âà 10 m/s¬≤.

2

Gaining Speed

As speed increases, collisions with air particles increase. This creates an upward frictional force called air resistance (or drag), which opposes the weight. The rate of acceleration starts to decrease.

3

Forces Balance

Eventually, the skydiver falls fast enough that the upward force of air resistance becomes equal to the downward force of weight. The forces are now balanced.

4

Terminal Velocity

With balanced forces, the resultant force is zero. According to Newton's laws, this means there is zero acceleration. The skydiver now falls at a constant, maximum speed called terminal velocity.

Important to remember

  • Don't confuse 'air resistance' with 'air pressure' ‚Äì they are different concepts!
  • A common misconception is that skydivers move upwards when they deploy their parachute. This is incorrect! They are actually decelerating rapidly to a new, much slower terminal velocity.
