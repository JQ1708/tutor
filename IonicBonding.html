Formation of Ions

  • An ion is an electrically charged atom or group of atoms formed by the loss or gain of electrons.
  • This loss or gain of electrons takes place to obtain a full outer shell of electrons.
  • The electronic structure of ions of elements in groups 1, 2, 3, 5, 6 and 7 will be the same as that of a noble gas (helium, neon, argon).
  • Negative ions are called anions and form when atoms gain electrons (more electrons than protons).
  • Positive ions are called cations and form when atoms lose electrons (more protons than electrons).
  • All metals lose electrons to other atoms to become positively charged ions.
  • All non-metals gain electrons from other atoms to become negatively charged ions.
Examiner Tip: The number of electrons that an atom gains or loses is the same as the charge (e.g., Mg loses 2 → Mg²⁺; Br gains 1 → Br⁻).

How to deduce the charge of an ion

  • Find the number of electrons in the outer electron shell.
  • Decide if it is easier for the atom to gain an electron or donate an electron (usually: fewer than four → donate; more than four → gain).
  • Atoms that gain electrons become negative ions; atoms that donate electrons form positive ions.
  • Learn the formulae of compound ions (made from more than one element).

Formulae for Ionic Compounds

  • Ionic compounds typically have no overall charge.
  • This means the size of any positively charged ion is cancelled by the size of any negatively charged ion.
  • Careful: Do not confuse this with an atom having no overall charge.
  • The formulae of simple ionic compounds can be determined if you know the charge on the ions.

Direct comparison

  • Compare the charges and see how many ions are needed to balance to zero.
  • Example: iron(II) sulfate
    • Fe²⁺ (2⁺) and SO₄²⁻ (2⁻)
    • Charges cancel: (+2) + (−2) = 0 → formula is FeSO₄.

Swap-and-drop method

  • Useful when the ions have different charges.
  • Careful: If ions have the same charge, reduce to the simplest whole-number ratio.
  • Example: copper(II) chloride → Cu²⁺ and Cl⁻ → formula CuCl₂.

Build-the-formula (practice)

Enter two ions with their charges (e.g., Fe 3 and Cl 1). Brackets will be used for polyatomic ions if you tick the box.

Examiner tip: For complex ions such as hydroxide, carbonate, or sulfate, include brackets if needed (e.g., Mg(OH)₂).

Worked example

The compound produced in the reaction between iron wool and chlorine contains Fe³⁺ and Cl⁻.

  1. Formula: FeCl₃ (by direct comparison or swap-and-drop).
  2. Name: iron chloride.

Ionic Bonds: Dot & Cross Diagrams

  • Ionic bonds can be represented diagrammatically using dot-and-cross diagrams.
  • Electrons from each atom should be shown as solid dots and crosses (with extra symbols if more than two atoms).
  • Use large square brackets around each ion and show the charge as a superscript on the right, outside the brackets.

Sodium chloride

The formula of sodium chloride is NaCl.

Magnesium oxide

Magnesium loses two electrons and oxygen gains two electrons. The formula is MgO.

For exams you need only show the outer electrons in dot & cross diagrams. You should be able to draw them for combinations from groups 1, 2, 3, 5, 6 and 7.

Ionic Compounds: Bonds, Structure & Properties

  • An ionic compound consists of a regular arrangement of alternating positive and negative ions which are tightly packed.
  • Between positive and negative ions are strong electrostatic forces of attraction which act in all directions.
  • These forces hold the compound together.

Ionic lattices

  • Thousands of positive and negative ions form a giant lattice structure.
  • Compounds with giant ionic lattice have high melting points.
  • Ionic compounds have high melting and boiling points because:
    • They have giant ionic lattices.
    • There are strong electrostatic forces between oppositely charged ions in all directions.
    • These forces need lots of energy to overcome.
  • The greater the charge on the ions, the stronger the electrostatic forces and the higher the melting point will be.
    Example: MgO (Mg²⁺ and O²⁻) has a higher melting point than NaCl (Na⁺ and Cl⁻).

Electrical Conductivity of Ionic Compounds

  • For electrical current to flow there must be freely moving charged particles (electrons or ions).
  • Ionic compounds are poor conductors in the solid state:
    • Ions are fixed in the lattice, so they cannot move and carry charge.
  • Ionic compounds are good conductors in the molten state or in solution:
    • When melted or dissolved, ions are able to move and carry charge.
Common mistake: Saying ionic compounds conduct because electrons move. Correct statement: the ions move and carry charge.
