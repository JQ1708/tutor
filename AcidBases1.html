Space for Blast Furnace Diagram

(a) Name the ore of iron which is mainly iron(III) oxide.

Tap to reveal answer

(b) Describe the reactions occurring in the blast furnace.

In your answer, include

  • two reasons for using coke in the blast furnace,
  • a chemical equation for the reduction of iron(III) oxide,
  • an explanation for using limestone in the blast furnace.
Tap to reveal answer

Coke (carbon) is used in the blast furnace for two main reasons. Firstly, coke reacts with oxygen from the hot air in an exothermic reaction to produce heat, which raises the temperature of the furnace. Secondly, coke acts as a reducing agent by producing carbon monoxide, which reduces the iron(III) oxide to iron.

The chemical equation for the reduction of iron(III) oxide by carbon monoxide is: Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂

Limestone (calcium carbonate) is added to the furnace to remove acidic impurities, such as silicon(IV) oxide (sand). The limestone thermally decomposes to form calcium oxide, which then reacts with the silicon(IV) oxide to form molten calcium silicate, also known as slag.

(c) (i) Describe the bonding in iron. Include a diagram in your answer.

Space for Metallic Bonding Diagram
Tap to reveal answer

Iron has metallic bonding, which consists of a regular lattice of positive ions (cations) surrounded by a 'sea' of delocalised electrons. There is a strong electrostatic attraction between the positive ions and the delocalised electrons, which holds the structure together.

(ii) Use your diagram in (c)(i) to explain why iron is malleable.

Tap to reveal answer

Iron is malleable because the layers of positive ions can slide over each other when a force is applied. The delocalised electrons can move with the ions, maintaining the metallic bond, so the structure does not shatter.

(iii) Iron containing a small amount of carbon is known as steel. Explain why steel is less malleable than iron.

Tap to reveal answer

In steel, the carbon atoms have a different size to the iron atoms. This disrupts the regular arrangement of the layers, making it more difficult for the layers to slide over each other.

(a) 8.88 g of calcium hydroxide and 7200 cm³ of chlorine gas are mixed together.

(i) How many moles is 8.88 g of calcium hydroxide?

Tap to reveal answer

Mᵣ of Ca(OH)₂ = 40 + (16 + 1) x 2 = 74

Moles = mass ⁄ Mᵣ = 8.88 ⁄ 74 = 0.12 mol

(ii) How many moles of chlorine gas is 7200 cm³?

Tap to reveal answer

Volume in dm³ = 7200 ⁄ 1000 = 7.2 dm³

Moles = volume ⁄ 24 = 7.2 ⁄ 24 = 0.3 mol

(iii) What is the maximum number of moles of calcium chlorate(V) that can be made from 8.88 g of calcium hydroxide and 7200 cm³ of chlorine gas?

Tap to reveal answer

From the equation, the mole ratio Ca(OH)₂ : Cl₂ is 6:6 or 1:1. We have 0.12 mol of Ca(OH)₂ and 0.3 mol of Cl₂. Therefore, Ca(OH)₂ is the limiting reactant.

The mole ratio Ca(OH)₂ : Ca(ClO₃)₂ is 6:1.

Moles of Ca(ClO₃)₂ = 0.12 ⁄ 6 = 0.02 mol

(iv) What is the maximum mass of calcium chlorate(V) that can be made from 8.88 g of calcium hydroxide and 7200 cm³ of chlorine gas?

Mass = moles x Mᵣ = 0.02 x 207 = 4.14 g
