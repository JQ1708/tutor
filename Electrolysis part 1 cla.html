🔬 Level 1: Electrolysis Principles

💡 PANIC Mnemonic: Positive is Anode, Negative Is Cathode

What is electrolysis?

The process of heating ionic compounds
The process in which a molten ionic compound is broken down by an electric current
The process of dissolving compounds in water
The process of mixing different chemicals

Why can't solid ionic compounds undergo electrolysis?

They are too hard
They have no free ions that can move and carry charge - ions are fixed in place
They don't contain ions
They are not conductive

What happens at each electrode?

🔴 CATHODE (Negative)

Cations are attracted here

REDUCTION occurs (gain electrons)

Produces: Metals or Hydrogen gas

🔵 ANODE (Positive)

Anions are attracted here

OXIDATION occurs (lose electrons)

Produces: Non-metals (except hydrogen)

🔥 Level 3: Molten Compounds

Virtual Experiment: Molten Lead(II) Bromide

🔴 ANODE (+)

Bromide ions (Br⁻) migrate here

Each loses one electron

2Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2e⁻

Brown bromine gas bubbles!

🔵 CATHODE (-)

Lead ions (Pb²⁺) migrate here

Each gains two electrons

Pb²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Pb

Grey lead metal deposits!

Binary Ionic Compound Rule:

📝 Binary ionic compounds always produce their corresponding elements:
Cathode = Metal, Anode = Non-metal

💧 Level 4: Aqueous Solutions

Electrolysis of Aqueous Sodium Chloride (Brine)

🔴 ANODE (+)

Chlorine Gas (Cl₂)

2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻

Test: Damp litmus paper

💧 SOLUTION

Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH)

Na⁺ + OH⁻ remain

Used for soap & detergents!

🔵 CATHODE (-)

Hydrogen Gas (H₂)

2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂

Test: Lit splint

🏭 Industrial Uses:

Chlorine: Bleach production | Hydrogen: Margarine production | NaOH: Soap & detergents

Why H⁺ instead of Na⁺ at cathode?

Sodium is heavier
H⁺ ions are less reactive than sodium ions
There are more H⁺ ions
Sodium doesn't conduct electricity

Electrolysis of Dilute Sulfuric Acid

🔴 ANODE (+)

Oxygen Gas (O₂)

4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻

Test: Glowing splint

🔵 CATHODE (-)

Hydrogen Gas (H₂)

2H⁺ + 2e⁻ → H₂

Test: Lit splint

📊 Volume Ratio: H₂ : O₂ = 2 : 1 (Twice as much hydrogen produced!)

🧬 Level 5: Advanced Aqueous Solutions (Extended Tier)

🌊 Water Dissociation

H₂O ⇌ H⁺ + OH⁻
Water always provides H⁺ and OH⁻ ions in aqueous solutions!

⚖️ Reactivity Series - Which gets discharged?

Most Reactive: K, Na, Ca, Mg, Al
HYDROGEN
Least Reactive: Cu, Ag
📝 Above hydrogen: H₂ gas produced at cathode
📝 Below hydrogen: Metal deposited at cathode

🧪 Concentration Effects at Anode

🔸 Concentrated Halide Solutions

Halogen gas produced

Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻ → Cl₂, Br₂, I₂

Example: Concentrated BaCl₂ → Cl₂ gas

🔹 Dilute Halide Solutions

Oxygen gas produced

4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻

Example: Dilute BaCl₂ → O₂ gas

⚗️ Copper Sulfate Electrolysis

With Graphite Electrodes:

🔴 ANODE (+)

OH⁻ discharged (not SO₄²⁻)

4OH⁻ → O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻

Oxygen gas bubbles!

🔵 CATHODE (-)

Cu²⁺ discharged (not H⁺)

Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu

Copper deposits!

🔄 Copper Electrodes Magic

📈 CATHODE GAINS MASS

Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu

Copper deposits on electrode

📉 ANODE LOSES MASS

Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻

Copper dissolves from electrode

⚖️ Mass gained = Mass lost

🔄 Cu²⁺ concentration stays constant!
