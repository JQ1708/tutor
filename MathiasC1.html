1.1 Photosynthesis: The Basics

This section covers the big idea: how plants make their own food. This process is called Photosynthesis, and it happens in plants (not animals!).

What's Needed? (The Reactants)

To make their food, plants need two main raw materials (reactants):

  • Carbon Dioxide: A gas taken from the air.
  • Water: Absorbed from the soil by the roots.

What's the Secret Ingredient?

Photosynthesis cannot happen at night. Why? Because it needs light energy (usually from the sun)!

This light energy is absorbed by a green pigment called chlorophyll, which is found inside tiny parts of the plant cell called chloroplasts.

What's Made? (The Products)

The "food" plants make is a type of sugar called glucose (a carbohydrate). Most of a plant's carbohydrate is made from carbon dioxide and water, not from nutrients in the soil.

A waste product is also released: Oxygen! This is the gas that animals (like us) breathe in.

The Word Equation

We can model photosynthesis using a word equation:

Carbon Dioxide   +   Water   →   Glucose   +   Oxygen

1.2 Investigating Photosynthesis

As scientists, we need to know how to plan investigations, identify hazards (dangers), and interpret our results (understand what they mean).

Practical 1: Oxygen from Pondweed

We can show photosynthesis releases oxygen using pondweed.

  • Observation: When the lamp is on, the pondweed produces bubbles of gas.
  • The Gas: This gas is Oxygen.
  • Test for Oxygen: You can test for this gas by collecting it in a test tube. Insert a glowing splint (a piece of wood that's glowing but not on fire). If it's oxygen, the splint will relight!
  • Variables:
    • Dependent Variable: What you measure. In this case, the amount of gas produced (e.g., number of bubbles per minute).
    • Independent Variable: What you change. You could change the light intensity by moving the lamp closer or further away.

Practical 2: Testing a Leaf for Starch

Glucose is often stored as starch. We can test if photosynthesis has happened by testing for starch using Iodine solution.

Hazard Warning: This practical uses flammable ethanol and boiling water. Always wear eye protection and use a water bath to heat the ethanol (never a direct flame!).

  1. Soften: Boil the leaf in water for a minute. This softens the leaf and stops all chemical reactions.
  2. Remove Chlorophyll: Boil the leaf in ethanol (in a water bath) for a few minutes. This removes the green chlorophyll.
  3. Wash: Wash the leaf in water to remove the ethanol and soften the now-brittle leaf.
  4. Test: Add iodine solution to the leaf.
  • Positive Result (Starch present): Iodine turns from orange-brown to blue-black.
  • Negative Result (No starch): Iodine stays orange-brown.

Application: You find few plants growing underneath water lilies because the lilies block the sunlight. Without light, the plants below cannot photosynthesise, produce glucose, or store starch.

1.3 Plant Transport: Moving Water

Plants need a transport system to move water from the roots to the leaves (for photosynthesis) and to move food (glucose) around.

Practical: Celery Stalk Investigation

This practical shows how water moves up the stem.

  • Setup: A celery stalk with leaves is placed in a glass of water mixed with blue food colouring.
  • Observation 1: The next morning, the leaves have turned blue.
  • Observation 2: When the stalk is cut, you see small blue circles inside.

Explanation

  • The blue circles are the Xylem Vessels.
  • The xylem is a network of tubes that transports water and minerals from the roots, up the stem, and into the leaves.
  • The leaves turned blue because of a process called Transpiration.
  • Transpiration is the evaporation of water from the surface of the leaves. This evaporation pulls water up through the xylem vessels (like sucking on a straw).
  • Hypothesis: If you cut the leaves off a celery stalk, less water will be taken in by the stalk. This is because you have removed the main surface for transpiration, reducing the "pull".

1.4 Plant Nutrition & Growth

Plants don't just need CO2 and water. They also need minerals from the soil for healthy growth. They get these using fertiliser.

Absorption

  • The specialised cells that absorb water and minerals from the soil are Root Hair Cells.
  • These cells have a large surface area to absorb water and minerals efficiently.

Why Minerals? The Role of Nitrates

  • If plants do not get enough nitrates, they have stunted growth (they don't grow properly).
  • Why? Plants use nitrates to make Amino Acids.
  • These Amino Acids are then joined together to build Proteins.
  • Proteins are essential building blocks for growth and repair of cells.

Summary: No Nitrates → No Amino Acids → No Proteins → No Growth

Autotrophs vs. Heterotrophs

This is a key difference in how organisms get their nutrition:

  • Water Lily (Plant): Is an Autotroph. This means it makes its own food (using photosynthesis).
  • Human (Animal): Is a Heterotroph. This means it must eat other organisms (plants or animals) to get its food and energy.
