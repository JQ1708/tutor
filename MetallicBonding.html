Metallic bonding

  • Metals consist of giant structures of atoms arranged in a regular pattern.
  • Within the metal lattice, the atoms lose their outer electrons and become positively charged metal ions.
  • The outer electrons no longer belong to any specific metal atom and are delocalised — they can move freely between the positive metal ions as a “sea of electrons”.
  • The metallic bond is the strong electrostatic attraction between the positive metal ions and the delocalised electrons.
  • This type of bonding occurs in metals and metal alloys (mixtures of metals).

What are the properties of metals?

  • Most metals have high melting and boiling points.
    • There are strong electrostatic forces of attraction between the positive metal ions and the negative delocalised electrons within the metal lattice structure.
    • These need lots of energy to be broken.
  • Metals conduct electricity because there are delocalised electrons available to move and carry charge.
  • Most metals are malleable — they can be hammered into shape — because the atoms/ions are arranged in layers which can slide over each other when a force is applied.

Examiner tips: Use the correct terminology. For malleability, refer to atoms/ions, not just “particles”.

The malleability of metals

When a force is applied, one layer of ions can slide over the layer beneath without breaking the structure, so the metal bends rather than shatters.

True or false? Malleability is possible because electrons are fixed in place.

Flip-to-learn flashcards

Metal lattice
A giant regular arrangement of metal ions after outer electrons are lost.
Delocalised electrons
Outer shell electrons not bound to a single atom; they move freely through the lattice.
Metallic bond
Strong electrostatic attraction between positive metal ions and delocalised electrons.
Malleable
Can be hammered into shape because layers of ions can slide over each other.
