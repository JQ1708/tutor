Question 30(b): Empirical Formula of Salvarsan

This problem requires calculating an empirical formula from mass percentage data.

Steps to Determine the Empirical Formula

Step 1: Determine the mass of each element in a 100 g sample. For arsenic, you'll first need to calculate its mass percentage from the given data (1.78 g in a 5.22 g sample).

Step 2: The mass percentages won't add up to 100%. The remaining mass is oxygen. Calculate the mass of oxygen.

Step 3: Convert the mass of each element into moles by dividing by its molar mass.

Step 4: Find the simplest whole-number ratio by dividing all the mole values by the smallest mole value you calculated.

Final Answer: The empirical formula is C6H7AsClNO.

Question 32: pH of a Solution Mixture

This question involves a neutralization reaction between a strong acid and a strong base to determine the final pH.

Steps to Calculate the Final pH

Step 1: Write a balanced chemical equation for the reaction: 2 HNO3 + Ca(OH)2 → Ca(NO3)2 + 2 H2O.

Step 2: Calculate the initial moles of H+ from the nitric acid.

Step 3: Calculate the initial moles of OH- from the calcium hydroxide. Remember that each mole of Ca(OH)2 produces two moles of OH-.

Step 4: Determine the limiting reactant and calculate the moles of the excess ion (either H+ or OH-) remaining after the reaction.

Step 5: Calculate the new total volume by adding the volumes of the two solutions.

Step 6: Find the concentration of the excess ion in the final solution.

Step 7: Calculate the pOH (if OH- is excess) or pH. If you find pOH, convert it to pH using the formula pH = 14 - pOH.

Final Answer: The pH of the final solution is 12.1.

Question 39: Titration of a Weed Killer

This involves analyzing titration data to verify the concentration of acetic acid in a commercial product.

Part (a): Calculate Average Titre

Step 1: For each titration, calculate the titre by subtracting the initial reading from the final reading.

Step 2: Identify the concordant titres (values that are very close to each other, typically within 0.10 mL). Exclude any outliers.

Step 3: Calculate the average of the concordant titres.

Final Answer for (a): The average titre is 19.43 mL.

Part (c): Verify Acetic Acid Concentration

Step 1: Calculate the moles of NaOH used in the titration using the average titre and the given NaOH concentration.

Step 2: Acetic acid and NaOH react in a 1:1 ratio. Determine the moles of acetic acid in the 20.00 mL diluted sample.

Step 3: Scale up to find the moles of acetic acid in the 250.0 mL volumetric flask.

Step 4: These moles came from the initial 5.00 mL of concentrate. Calculate the concentration (in mol L-1) of the original weed killer.

Step 5: Convert this concentration to g/L using the molar mass of acetic acid.

Step 6: Calculate the acceptable concentration range (295 g/L ± 5.00%).

Step 7: Compare your calculated value with the acceptable range to see if it matches.

Final Answer for (c): The experimental value is 276 g/L, which is outside the acceptable range of 280.25 - 309.75 g/L. Therefore, it does not match the label.

Question 40: Lithium Extraction Chemistry

These calculations involve stoichiometry, limiting reactants, and percentage yield in an industrial extraction process.

Part (a): Concentration of Remaining Al3+

Step 1: Calculate the initial moles of Rb+ and Al3+ in the 5.0 L solution from their given concentrations.

Step 2: The problem states all Rb+ precipitates. Use the 1:1 mole ratio from the equation to find how many moles of Al3+ reacted with the Rb+.

Step 3: Subtract the moles of reacted Al3+ from the initial moles of Al3+ to find the moles remaining.

Step 4: Convert the remaining moles of Al3+ back into a concentration in g/L for the 5.0 L solution.

Final Answer for (a): The concentration of Al3+ remaining is 1.4 g L-1.

Part (b): Sufficiency of K2SO4

Step 1: Calculate the total moles of K+ ions added from the K2SO4 solution. Note the '2' in K2SO4.

Step 2: The moles of K+ required to precipitate the remaining Al3+ is equal to the moles of Al3+ you calculated in part (a) (due to the 1:1 ratio in the new equation).

Step 3: Compare the moles of K+ added to the moles of K+ required.

Final Answer for (b): Yes, sufficient K2SO4 was added.

Part (d): Percentage Yield of Li2CO3

Step 1: Calculate the initial mass of Li+ in the 5.0 L of solution, and then convert this to moles.

Step 2: Determine the theoretical moles of Li2CO3 that could be produced. Note that it takes 2 moles of Li+ to make 1 mole of Li2CO3.

Step 3: Convert the theoretical moles of Li2CO3 to a mass. This is your theoretical yield.

Step 4: The actual yield is given as 46.7 g. Use the formula: % Yield = (Actual Yield / Theoretical Yield) × 100%.

Final Answer for (d): The percentage yield is 82%.
