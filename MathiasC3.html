1. Types of Variation

Variation means the differences between individuals. There are two main types. Tap the cards to learn about them!

Continuous Variation

A characteristic that changes gradually over a range of values.


Examples:

  • Height
  • Arm Span
  • Foot Size

Best shown by a Histogram.

Discontinuous Variation

A characteristic that has distinct categories or options.


Examples:

  • Ability to roll tongue
  • Blood Group (A, B, AB, O)

Best shown by a Bar Chart.

Where does variation come from?

Inherited Variation

Caused by genes passed down from parents.


Examples:

  • Blood Group
  • Natural Eye Colour
  • A hydrangea's natural leaf shape
Environmental Variation

Caused by the surroundings or lifestyle.


Examples:

  • Hydrangea leaves turning yellow (due to lack of magnesium in soil)
  • Hydrangea flowers turning blue (due to acidic soil)

2. Cells and Genetics

Let's zoom into the cell to see where your characteristics come from. Click the (+) to reveal the information.

1. The Nucleus

The Nucleus is a part of the cell that contains the genetic information. It's like the cell's control center.

2. Chromosomes

Inside the nucleus, the genetic information is stored on structures called Chromosomes.

Human body cells have 23 pairs of chromosomes (46 total).

3. DNA

Chromosomes are long, coiled strands of a molecule called DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid).

Think of DNA as a giant instruction manual for building and running your body.

4. Genes

A Gene is a short section of DNA.

Each gene carries the instructions (code) for one specific characteristic (like making a protein for eye colour).

Space for Diagram:

A diagram showing the relationship: Cell → Nucleus → Chromosome → DNA → Gene.

Sex Chromosomes

One pair of chromosomes determines your biological sex:

  • Females have two X chromosomes: XX
  • Males have one X and one Y chromosome: XY

3. Inheritance & Gametes

Inherited variation is caused by the different genes individuals inherit from their parents. This happens through special cells called Gametes. Tap the cards!

What are Gametes?

A Gamete is the general name for a sex cell.

They contain HALF the number of chromosomes (23 single chromosomes in humans).

  • Female gamete = Egg cell (always has an X chromosome)
  • Male gamete = Sperm cell (has an X or a Y chromosome)
Fertilisation

This is the process where the nucleus of a sperm cell (male gamete) joins with the nucleus of an egg cell (female gamete).

Zygote & Embryo

A fertilised egg cell is called a Zygote. It now has the full 23 pairs of chromosomes.

The zygote divides many times to become an Embryo.

A Healthy Diet

A pregnant woman needs a healthy diet to provide the nutrients and energy needed for the embryo to grow and develop properly.

4. Natural Selection

This is the theory (from Charles Darwin) that explains how species change (evolve) over a long time. Let's use the giraffe ancestor example. Navigate the slides below!

Step 1: Variation

In a population of giraffe ancestors, there was variation. Some had slightly longer necks than others, just by chance (due to random gene mutations).

Step 2: Competition (Struggle)

All the giraffes competed for the same food (leaves on trees). If food became scarce (e.g., leaves only on high branches), not all would survive.

Step 3: Survival of the Fittest

The giraffes with slightly longer necks were better adapted. They could reach more food, so they were more likely to survive.

Step 4: Inheritance

The surviving (long-necked) giraffes reproduced. They passed on their genes for longer necks to their offspring.

Step 5: Gradual Change (Evolution)

Over thousands of years (many generations), this process repeated. The advantageous gene became more common, and the average neck length of the population increased.
