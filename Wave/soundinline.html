1. The Nature of Sound Waves

Sound is all around us, but what is it fundamentally? Let's explore its properties.

What is Sound?

The fundamental definition.

  • Produced by vibrating sources.
  • A longitudinal wave.
  • Requires a medium to travel through.
  • Cannot travel through a vacuum as there are no particles to vibrate.

Longitudinal Motion

What makes it longitudinal?

The oscillations (vibrations) of particles are parallel to the direction of energy transfer.

Compressions & Rarefactions

The "squash and stretch" of sound.

Compressions: Regions where particles are close together (high pressure).

Rarefactions: Regions where particles are spread apart (low pressure).

Sound Transmission

How does sound travel?

Vibrating particles pass on their energy to neighbouring particles. This is why sound can cause objects like a drinking glass to vibrate and even shatter if the frequency matches its natural frequency.

2. The Speed of Sound

Sound travels at different speeds in different materials. How can we measure it in the air?

Measuring the Speed

  1. Two people stand a large, measured distance apart (e.g., >100m).
  2. Person A has cymbals. Person B has a stopwatch.
  3. Person B starts the stopwatch on seeing the cymbals clash.
  4. Person B stops the stopwatch on hearing the sound.
  5. Calculate speed using:
Speed = Distance / Time

Repeat and average for accuracy. A larger distance minimises reaction time error.

Speed in Different Media

Does the medium matter?

Yes! Sound travels fastest in solids and slowest in gases.

Solid > Liquid > Gas

This is because the particles are closer together in solids, allowing vibrations to be passed on more quickly.

3. Pitch, Loudness & Hearing Range

We can visualise sound waves using a Cathode Ray Oscilloscope (CRO) to see how their properties relate to what we hear.

Pitch

The "highness" or "lowness" of a sound.

Pitch is determined by the frequency of the wave.

High Frequency = High Pitch

Low Frequency = Low Pitch

Loudness

The intensity or volume of a sound.

Loudness is determined by the amplitude of the wave.

Large Amplitude = Loud Sound

Small Amplitude = Quiet Sound

Human Hearing Range

What can we actually hear?

20 Hz to 20,000 Hz

Frequencies above this range are called Ultrasound. Frequencies below this are called Infrasound.

4. Ultrasound

Sound with a frequency too high for humans to hear (> 20,000 Hz). Its ability to reflect off boundaries makes it incredibly useful.
