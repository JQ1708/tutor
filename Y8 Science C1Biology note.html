Introduction: Why We Need Oxygen

Respiration is one of the essential characteristics of all living things. It's not just about breathing! Respiration is a series of chemical reactions that happens inside every single living cell to release energy from food.

The main type is aerobic respiration.

This process uses oxygen and produces carbon dioxide as a waste product.

Glucose + Oxygen → Carbon Dioxide + Water (+ Energy Released)

1.1 The Human Respiratory System

The respiratory system is the group of organs that work together to get oxygen from the air into our body. Let's explore the path air takes. Tap the cards to flip them!

Space for diagram showing the human respiratory system

Trachea (Windpipe)

The main tube that carries air from the throat towards the lungs. It is held open by incomplete, C-shaped rings of cartilage.

Bronchi & Bronchioles

The trachea splits into two bronchi (singular: bronchus), one for each lung. These then branch into smaller and smaller tubes called bronchioles.

Air Sacs (Alveoli)

Tiny bags at the end of the bronchioles where gas exchange happens. There are millions in each lung!

Lungs

The main organs of the respiratory system. They are spongy, elastic organs filled with the network of bronchioles and air sacs.

Larynx (Voicebox)

Found at the top of the trachea. It contains the vocal cords, which vibrate when stretched to produce sound as air passes over them.

Cleaning the Air

The trachea and bronchi are lined with cells that make sticky mucus to trap dirt and pathogens. Tiny hairs called cilia sweep this mucus upwards to be swallowed.

1.2 Gas Exchange

This is the crucial process where oxygen enters the blood and carbon dioxide leaves it by diffusion. It all happens in the air sacs (alveoli).

Space for diagram of Gas Exchange at an Alveolus

How are Air Sacs Adapted for Gas Exchange?

  • 1️⃣
    Large surface area: There are millions of them, creating a total surface area as big as a tennis court! This allows a lot of gas to diffuse at once.
  • 2️⃣
    Thin walls: The walls of the air sacs and the capillaries are only one cell thick. This creates a very short distance for the gases to travel.
  • 3️⃣
    Moist Surface: The air sacs have a moist lining, allowing oxygen to dissolve before it diffuses into the blood.
  • 4️⃣
    Good blood supply: A dense network of capillaries surrounds each air sac. Blood constantly flows, maintaining a steep concentration gradient for efficient diffusion.

1.3 Breathing

Important: Breathing (also called ventilation) and respiration are NOT the same thing! Breathing is the physical process of moving air in and out of the lungs. Respiration is the chemical reaction that releases energy in cells.

Breathing In (Inhalation)

  1. The intercostal muscles contract, pulling the rib cage up and out.
  2. The diaphragm contracts and flattens.
  3. These actions increase the volume inside the chest.
  4. This decreases the pressure inside the chest.
  5. Air is drawn into the lungs.

Breathing Out (Exhalation)

  1. The intercostal muscles relax, letting the rib cage fall down and in.
  2. The diaphragm relaxes and becomes dome-shaped.
  3. These actions decrease the volume inside the chest.
  4. This increases the pressure inside the chest.
  5. Air is forced out of the lungs.

1.4 The Effects of Smoking

Cigarette smoke contains thousands of chemicals, many of which are harmful. Let's look at the main culprits.

Tar

A sticky, black substance that collects in the lungs. It is a carcinogen (causes cancer). Tar also paralyses and damages the cilia, leading to a build-up of mucus containing trapped dirt and bacteria. This can lead to a 'smoker's cough' and infections.

Nicotine

An addictive drug that makes it hard to stop smoking. It increases heart rate and blood pressure, which can lead to heart disease.

Carbon Monoxide

A poisonous gas that binds irreversibly to haemoglobin in red blood cells. This reduces the ability of the blood to carry oxygen, making the heart work harder to supply the body with enough oxygen.

Smoking-Related Diseases

Space for comparative diagram of Healthy Alveoli vs. Damaged Alveoli (Emphysema)
  • Lung Cancer: Uncontrolled growth of cells in the lungs, often caused by the carcinogens in tar.
  • Bronchitis: Inflammation of the bronchi. Damaged cilia cause mucus (with trapped bacteria) to build up, leading to persistent coughing and infection.
  • Emphysema: An irreversible disease where the walls of the air sacs are broken down, reducing the surface area for gas exchange and causing severe breathlessness.
  • Heart Disease: Smoking increases the risk of heart attacks and strokes. Nicotine raises blood pressure, and carbon monoxide forces the heart to work harder.
