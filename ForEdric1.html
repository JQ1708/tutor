Question 37: Hydrocarbon Analysis

This question involves determining the empirical and molecular formulas of a hydrocarbon from combustion data and gas properties.

Part (a): Determine the Empirical Formula

Step 1: Calculate the moles of carbon (C) from the mass of carbon dioxide (CO2).

Step 2: Calculate the moles of hydrogen (H) from the mass of water (H2O).

Step 3: Find the simplest whole-number ratio of moles of C to moles of H to determine the empirical formula.

Final Answer for (a): The empirical formula is CH2.

Part (b): Determine the Molecular Formula

Step 1: Use the Ideal Gas Law, PV = nRT, to calculate the number of moles (n) of the hydrocarbon gas. Remember to convert temperature to Kelvin and pressure to Pascals if using R = 8.314 J/(mol·K).

Step 2: Calculate the molar mass (M) of the hydrocarbon using the formula M = mass / moles.

Step 3: Find the ratio of the calculated molar mass to the mass of the empirical formula. This will give you the multiplier to find the molecular formula.

Final Answer for (b): The molar mass is approximately 56.4 g/mol, and the molecular formula is C4H8.

Question 38: Groundwater Treatment

This question is about a stoichiometry problem involving a chemical reaction to remove iron from groundwater. The key is to identify the limiting reactant.

2 Fe(HCO3)2 + Cl2 + Ca(HCO3)2 → 2 Fe(OH)3 + CaCl2 + 6 CO2

Part (a): Calculate Mass of Precipitate

Step 1: Convert the mass of each reactant (Cl2, Fe(HCO3)2, and Ca(HCO3)2) into moles. Don't forget to convert milligrams to grams for iron(II) hydrogencarbonate.

Step 2: Use the mole ratios from the balanced equation to determine which reactant is the limiting reactant. This is the reactant that will be completely consumed first.

Step 3: Based on the moles of the limiting reactant, calculate the moles of iron(III) hydroxide (Fe(OH)3) that can be produced.

Step 4: Convert the moles of Fe(OH)3 to mass using its molar mass.

Final Answer for (a): The mass of iron(III) hydroxide precipitated is 21.1 g.

Part (b): Calculate Concentration of Calcium Chloride

Step 1: Using the moles of the limiting reactant found in part (a), determine the moles of calcium chloride (CaCl2) produced based on the stoichiometry of the reaction.

Step 2: Calculate the concentration of CaCl2 by dividing the moles of CaCl2 by the total volume of the solution in Litres.

Final Answer for (b): The concentration of calcium chloride is 3.29 x 10-6 mol L-1.
