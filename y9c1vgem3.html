Exercise 3.2 & 3.3

Heat, Temperature & Conservation of Energy.

Answers

3.2: Heat is total energy, temperature is average energy. Heating a solid makes particles vibrate faster. A larger block has more heat at the same temperature.

3.3: Energy is conserved (cannot be created/destroyed). Input energy = output energy. Sankey diagram for a device should show useful and wasted energy outputs summing to 100%.

Exercise 3.4 - 3.6

Thermal Energy Transfer & Cooling.

Answers

3.4: Thermal energy transfers from higher to lower temperature.

3.5: Conduction (solids), Convection (fluids), Radiation (can travel through vacuum). Metals are good conductors; wood is an insulator. White surfaces reflect radiation.

3.6: Evaporation (liquid to gas) causes cooling because the highest-energy particles escape, lowering the average energy (temperature) of the remaining liquid. A fan speeds up evaporation.

Unit 5: Reactivity

Topic 5.1 - 5.5

Exercise 5.1 & 5.2

Reactivity and Displacement Reactions.

Answers

5.1: A more reactive metal will displace a less reactive metal from its salt solution (e.g., Zinc + Copper Sulfate â Zinc Sulfate + Copper). You can use displacement reactions to determine a reactivity series.

5.2: The reactivity series can be used to predict reactions. Carbon is used to extract iron from its ore because it is more reactive than iron. The Thermite reaction (aluminium + iron oxide) is a displacement reaction that produces molten iron.

Exercise 5.3 & 5.4

Making Salts.

Answers

5.3: Salts are made from acids: Hydrochloric acid â chlorides, Sulfuric acid â sulfates, Nitric acid â nitrates. A metal more reactive than hydrogen will react with an acid to make a salt and hydrogen gas.

5.4: Other ways to make salts: Acid + Base (e.g., copper oxide), Acid + Carbonate (e.g., copper carbonate), Acid + Alkali (titration). Steps include reaction, filtration (to remove excess solid), and evaporation/crystallisation.

Exercise 5.5, Q1-10

Rearranging Atoms (Conservation of Mass).

Answers

Atoms are rearranged in a chemical reaction, not created or destroyed. The law of conservation of mass states that the total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products. If mass seems to be lost, it's often because a gas has escaped. If mass seems to be gained, it's often because a gas from the air (like oxygen) has reacted.

Unit 6: Sound and space

Topic 6.1 - 6.5

Exercise 6.1 & 6.2

Loudness, Pitch & Interference of Sound.

Answers

6.1: Loudness is related to amplitude (bigger amplitude = louder). Pitch is related to frequency (higher frequency = higher pitch).

6.2: Interference happens when waves meet. Constructive interference (reinforcement) makes sound louder. Destructive interference (cancellation) makes sound quieter or silent.

Exercise 6.3 & 6.4

Formation of the Moon & Nebulae.

Answers

6.3: The leading theory for the Moon's formation is the giant-impact hypothesis (collision theory): a Mars-sized object called Theia collided with early Earth, and the debris formed the Moon.

6.4: A nebula is a giant cloud of dust and gas (mostly hydrogen) in space. Some are stellar nurseries where stars are born due to gravitational collapse.

Exercise 6.5, Q1-3

Tectonics.

Answers

Tectonic plates are large pieces of Earth's crust that move slowly. This movement is driven by convection currents in the mantle. Evidence includes fossil distribution, earthquake/volcano patterns, and magnetic striping on the ocean floor. This movement causes continental drift.

Answers

7.1: Chromosomes are found in the nucleus and are made of DNA. Genes are sections of DNA that contain instructions for traits.

Exercise 7.3 & 7.4

Variation & Natural Selection.

Answers

7.3: Variation refers to the differences between individuals in a species. It can be recorded and displayed in tables and charts (e.g., bar charts, frequency diagrams).

7.4: Natural selection is the process where organisms with variations best suited to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce. They pass on these advantageous genes, leading to evolution over time (e.g., camouflage, woolly mammoths' fur).

Unit 8: Rates of reaction

Topic 8.1 - 8.4

Exercise 8.1, Q1-7

Measuring and explaining the rate of reaction.

Answers

The rate of reaction is fastest at the start and slows down as reactants are used up. This can be seen from the gradient of a graph of product formed vs. time. The reaction stops when one reactant is completely used. The rate decreases because the concentration of reactants decreases, leading to fewer collisions per second.

Exercise 8.2, 8.3 & 8.4

Factors affecting reaction rate.

Answers

Surface Area (8.2): Increasing surface area (e.g., powder vs. lump) increases reaction rate because more reactant particles are exposed for collisions.

Temperature (8.3): Increasing temperature increases reaction rate because particles move faster (more frequent collisions) and have more energy (more successful collisions).

Concentration (8.4): Increasing concentration increases reaction rate because there are more reactant particles in the same volume, leading to more frequent collisions.

Answers

9.1: In a parallel circuit, there is more than one path for the current. The total current from the source is the sum of the currents in the separate branches.

9.2: In a parallel circuit, the voltage across each branch is the same as the voltage of the source.

Practical Circuits (Variable Resistors).

Answers

A variable resistor allows you to change the resistance in a circuit. Increasing the resistance will decrease the current (for a fixed voltage), which can be used to dim a lamp or change the speed of a motor. They can be connected in series or parallel depending on the desired effect.
