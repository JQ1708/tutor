Question 35(c): Nicotine Patch Analysis

This problem involves a titration calculation to determine the dosage of nicotine in a patch.

C10H14N2 + 2 CH3COOH2+ → C10H16N22+ + 2 CH3COOH

Steps to Identify Nicotine Dosage

Step 1: Calculate the moles of the acid titrant (CH3COOH2+) used, using n = cV.

Step 2: Use the mole ratio from the balanced equation (1 mole nicotine : 2 moles acid) to find the moles of nicotine in the 20.0 mL aliquot.

Step 3: Scale this up to find the total moles of nicotine in the 100.0 mL solution (multiply by 5, since 100/20 = 5).

Step 4: Calculate the total mass of nicotine extracted from all patches using its molar mass (C10H14N2).

Step 5: Divide the total mass of nicotine by the number of patches (14) to find the mass per patch.

Step 6: Convert the mass to milligrams (mg) to identify the correct dosage (7, 14, or 21 mg).

Final Answer: The boxes contain 21 mg patches.

Question 36: Glycoluril Formula Determination

This is a two-part problem to find the empirical formula from combustion and titration data, and then the molecular formula using gas properties.

Part (a): Determine the Empirical Formula

Step 1 (Carbon): Calculate moles of C from the mass of CO2 produced.

Step 2 (Hydrogen): Calculate moles of H from the mass of H2O produced.

Step 3 (Nitrogen): This is a back-titration. First, find the initial moles of H2SO4. Second, find the moles of NaOH used to neutralize the excess acid. Third, calculate moles of H2SO4 that reacted with NaOH. Fourth, subtract this from the initial moles of H2SO4 to find the moles that reacted with NH3. Finally, use the mole ratio (2 NH3 : 1 H2SO4) to find moles of NH3, which equals the moles of N in the sample.

Step 4 (Oxygen): Calculate the mass of C, H, and N in the 2.30 g sample. Subtract these masses from 2.30 g to find the mass of O, then convert it to moles.

Step 5 (Ratio): Find the simplest whole-number mole ratio of C:H:N:O.

Final Answer for (a): The empirical formula is C2H3N2O.

Part (b): Determine the Molecular Formula

Step 1: Use the Ideal Gas Law, PV = nRT, to find the moles (n) of the vaporized glycoluril. Remember to use Kelvin for temperature and kPa for pressure.

Step 2: Calculate the molar mass using M = mass / moles.

Step 3: Calculate the mass of the empirical formula (C2H3N2O).

Step 4: Find the ratio of the molar mass to the empirical formula mass. The result is the multiplier for the empirical formula subscripts.

Final Answer for (b): The molecular formula is C4H6N4O2.

Question 37: Battery Cathode Recycling

These calculations involve stoichiometry to determine if a reaction goes to completion and to find the mass percentage of an element.

Part (c): Will the Cathodes Dissolve Completely?

Step 1: Calculate the mass of cobalt (Co) in the 0.531 kg (531 g) cathode using the given percentage (57.29%).

Step 2: Convert the mass of Co to moles. Based on the formula LiCoO2, the moles of LiCoO2 are equal to the moles of Co.

Step 3: Calculate the mass of LiCoO2. Then, find the mass of manganese (Mn) by subtracting the mass of LiCoO2 from the total cathode mass (531 g).

Step 4: Convert the mass of Mn to moles.

Step 5: Use the reaction stoichiometry (2 moles of LiCoO2 react with 1 mole of Mn) to determine the limiting reactant. Compare the required mole ratio with the actual mole ratio you have.

Final Answer for (c): No, the cathodes will not dissolve completely because there is not enough Manganese (Mn) to react with all the Lithium Cobalt Oxide (LiCoO2).

Part (d): Calculate Mass Percentage of Lithium

Step 1: From your work in part (c), you have the moles of Co, which is equal to the moles of LiCoO2. The moles of Lithium (Li) are the same.

Step 2: Convert the moles of Li to mass using its molar mass.

Step 3: Calculate the percentage by mass of Li in the total cathode mass (531 g) using the formula: % Mass = (mass of Li / total mass) × 100%.

Final Answer for (d): The percentage by mass of lithium is 6.74%.
