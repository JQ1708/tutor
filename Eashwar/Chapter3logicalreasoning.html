Part A: Statement or Not a Statement?

Guidance: A sentence is a 'statement' if you can definitively say it's either TRUE or FALSE. If it's a question, an expression with variables (like 5x-3=x-5), or an instruction, it's not a statement.

1. 12 ÷ 6 = 3: Can you determine if this is true or false? Yes. So it is a ?.

2. 5x - 3 = x - 5: Can you determine if this is true or false without knowing x? No. So it is ? a statement.

3. 9m - 3p: Is this a complete sentence that can be true or false? No, it's an expression. So it is ? a statement.

4. 25 is a multiple of 5: Can you determine if this is true or false? Yes. So it is a ?.

1. 25 - 8 × 3 = 1: First, calculate 8 × 3 = ?. Then, 25 - ? = ?. The statement is False.

2. 1000 is a perfect square: Find a whole number that, when squared, equals 1000. For example, 302=900, 312=961, 322=1024. No whole number works. Statement is False.

3. 81 is a multiple of 3: Calculate 81 ÷ 3 = ?. Since it's a whole number, the statement is True.

4. (23)2 = 32: Use index laws: (23)2 = 23×2 = 2? = ?. This is not 32. Statement is False.

5. 2 + 1 < 5: Calculate 2 + 1 = ?. Is 3 less than 5? Yes. Statement is True.

6. 5 is a factor of 10: Calculate 10 ÷ 5 = ?. Since it's a whole number, the statement is True.

Gerak Kerja 19: Quantifiers and Negation

Part A: True or False

1. All octagons have eight equal sides: Is this true for ALL octagons, or only regular octagons? An octagon just needs 8 sides, not necessarily equal. Statement is False.

2. All acute angles are less than 90°: This is the definition of an acute angle. Statement is True.

Part B: Complete with "All" or "Some"

1. ... multiples of 5 have the digit 0: Think of 15 or 25. Not all do. So, use Some.

2. ... even numbers are perfect squares: Think of 4 (yes) but also 6 (no). So, use Some.

3. ... trapeziums have two parallel sides: This is the definition of a trapezium. So, use All.

4. ... odd numbers are divisible by 3: Think of 9 (yes) but also 5 (no). So, use Some.

Part C: Form a Negation (~p)

1. p: Perfect squares are negative numbers. (False) -> ~p: Perfect squares are not negative numbers. (Truth Value: True)

2. p: Hexagons have 6 sides. (True) -> ~p: Hexagons do not have 6 sides. (Truth Value: False)

3. p: All multiples of 4 are multiples of 2. (True) -> ~p: Some multiples of 4 are not multiples of 2. (Truth Value: False)

4. p: 8 L is equal to 800 ml. (False, 1L=1000ml) -> ~p: 8 L is not equal to 800 ml. (Truth Value: True)

Gerak Kerja 20: Compound Statements

Part B: Truth Value of Compound Statements

1. 64 is a multiple of 16 and a perfect square.

p: 64 is a multiple of 16 (True). q: 64 is a perfect square (True). "and" requires both to be true. So the statement is (True).

2. ∅ ∈ {2, 3} or 4 ∈ {5, 6}.

p: ∅ ∈ {2, 3} (False). q: 4 ∈ {5, 6} (False). "or" requires at least one to be true. Both are false, so the statement is (False).

3. 33 < 9 and 2000 = 2 × 103.

p: 33 < 9 (False, 27 is not < 9). q: 2000 = 2 × 103 (True). "and" requires both to be true. One is false, so the statement is (False).

4. 32 is a multiple of 4 or 6.

p: 32 is a multiple of 4 (True). q: 32 is a multiple of 6 (False). "or" requires at least one to be true. p is true, so the statement is (True).

Gerak Kerja 22: Converse, Inverse, and Contrapositive

Example: If 0.5 kg = 500 g, then ½ kg = 500 g.

Implication: True (p is True, q is True)
Converse: If ½ kg = 500 g, then 0.5 kg = 500 g. (q is True, p is True) -> True
Inverse: If 0.5 kg ≠ 500 g, then ½ kg ≠ 500 g. (~p is False, ~q is False) -> True
Contrapositive: If ½ kg ≠ 500 g, then 0.5 kg ≠ 500 g. (~q is False, ~p is False) -> True

1. If 2 and 8 are even numbers, then the product of 2 and 8 is an odd number.

Implication: False (p is True, q is False)
Converse: If the product ... is an odd number, then 2 and 8 are even numbers. (q is False, p is True) -> True
Inverse: If 2 or 8 are not even numbers, then the product ... is not an odd number. (~p is False, ~q is True) -> True
Contrapositive: If the product ... is not an odd number, then 2 or 8 are not even numbers. (~q is True, ~p is False) -> False

2. If cos 90° = 1, then sin 90° = 1.

Implication: True (p is False: cos 90=0, q is True)
Converse: If sin 90° = 1, then cos 90° = 1. (q is True, p is False) -> False
Inverse: If cos 90° ≠ 1, then sin 90° ≠ 1. (~p is True, ~q is False) -> False
Contrapositive: If sin 90° ≠ 1, then cos 90° ≠ 1. (~q is False, ~p is True) -> True

Gerak Kerja 23: Counter-Example

Part A: Provide a counter-example if false.

1. 4 and 6 are the factors of 24. (p: 4 is a factor of 24 (True). q: 6 is a factor of 24 (True). Statement is True. No counter-example needed.)

2. 3 or 12 is a multiple of 6. (p: 3 is a multiple of 6 (False). q: 12 is a multiple of 6 (True). Statement is True. No counter-example needed.)

3. All prime numbers are even number. (False. Counter-example: 3 is a prime number but it is not even.)

Part B: Write statement and find truth value/counter-example.

1. All multiples of 2 are multiples of 4. (Negation)

Negation: Some multiples of 2 are not multiples of 4. (True. Example: 6 is a multiple of 2 but not 4).

2. If y < 6, then y < 10. (Converse)

Converse: If y < 10, then y < 6. (False. Counter-example: y = 8. 8 is less than 10 but not less than 6).

Gerak Kerja 24: Deductive and Inductive Arguments

Part A: Deductive or Inductive?

1. All rhombuses have... EFGH is a rhombus. Thus... (Goes from a general rule to a specific case. This is Deductive.)

2. (3x1)-2=1, (3x4)-2=10... Thus, the sequence is... (Goes from specific examples to form a general rule. This is Inductive.)

Part B: Validity and Soundness

1. All multiples of 3 are multiples of 9...

Valid form. But Premise 1 is false (e.g., 6). So, Valid but Not Sound.

2. All positive integers are odd numbers...

Valid form. But Premise 1 is false (e.g., 2). So, Valid but Not Sound.

3. If m is divisible by 4... 6 is not divisible by 4. Conclusion: 6 is not divisible by 2.

This is in the form: If p, then q. Not p. Conclusion: Not q. This is an Invalid form (Denying the Antecedent). Therefore, it is also Not Sound.

Gerak Kerja 25: Forming Deductive Conclusions

1. P1: If x is an even number, then x+1 is an odd number. P2: 6 is an even number.

Conclusion: 6+1 is an odd number.

2. P1: All cubes have 6 square faces. P2: Solid H is a cube.

Conclusion: Solid H has 6 square faces.

3. P1: If x3=125, then x=5. P2: x ≠ 5.

Conclusion: x3 ≠ 125.

4. P1: If radius is 7cm, then area is 154cm2. P2: Area of circle A is not 154cm2.

Conclusion: The radius of circle A is not 7 cm.

Gerak Kerja 26: Forming Deductive Premises

1. P1: All factors of 21 are factors of 42. P2: ?. Conclusion: 7 is a factor of 42.

Premise 2 must link 7 to the first part of P1. P2: 7 is a factor of 21.

2. P1: ?. P2: If 2y-1=9, then y=5. Conclusion: 2y-1 ≠ 9.

This is the form "not q -> not p". P1 must be "not q". P1: y ≠ 5.

3. P1: ?. P2: x is not a multiple of 6. Conclusion: x is not a multiple of 18.

This is "not q -> not p". So P1 must be "if p then q". P1: If x is a multiple of 18, then x is a multiple of 6.

4. P1: ?. P2: 12 is an even number. Conclusion: 12 is divisible by 2.

P1 must be a general rule. P1: All even numbers are divisible by 2.

5. P1: ?. P2: If k≥7 then k+3>10. Conclusion: 9+3>10.

This is "p -> q". P1 must be "p". P1: 9 ≥ 7.

Gerak Kerja 28: Problem Solving

1. Balls in boxes: 2, 5, 10...

(a) Pattern is n2 + 1 for n=1,2,3...
(b) For box 6: 62 + 1 = 36 + 1 = 37.
(c) n2 + 1 = 122 -> n2 = 121 -> n = 11.

2. Team Scores

Let G, B, R, Y be the scores. G=B+50, R=G-15, R=Y+15, B=Y-20.
From G=B+50 and R=G-15, we get R = (B+50)-15 = B+35. So Red > Blue.
From R=Y+15, we get Red > Yellow.
From B=Y-20, we get Yellow > Blue.
Comparing R and G, R = G-15, so Green > Red.
Order: Green > Red > Yellow > Blue.
