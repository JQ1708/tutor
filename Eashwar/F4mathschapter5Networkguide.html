General Guidance for Drawing Graphs

Step 1: Draw the vertices. Start by placing dots on your paper for each vertex (e.g., A, B, C...). The initial placement doesn't have to be perfect; you can adjust as you go.

Step 2: Draw the edges. Read the list of edges or the description. For each edge, draw a line connecting the two specified vertices. For directed graphs (like the food chain in Q7), add an arrow to the line to show the direction.

Example for Q3: E = {(B,C), (B,D), (A,C), (A,D), (C,D)}. First, draw four points and label them A, B, C, D. Then draw a line from B to C, from B to D, and so on, until all five edges are drawn.

Question 6

(6) Given E = {(A,B), (B,C), (C,A), (C,D), (D,E), (C,F)}. Determine the degree of vertex A, C, and F.

Finding the Degree

The degree of a vertex is the number of edges connected to it. Count the number of times each vertex appears in the list of edges.

Degree of A (d(A)): A appears in (A,B) and (C,A). So, d(A) = 2.

Degree of C (d(C)): C appears in (B,C), (C,A), (C,D), and (C,F). So, d(C) = 4.

Degree of F (d(F)): F appears in (C,F). So, d(F) = 1.

Question 8

(8) Determine the number of edges for a graph with 10 vertices, where each vertex has a degree of 6.

Using the Sum of Degrees Formula

Sum of Degrees = 2 x (Number of Edges)

Step 1: Find the sum of degrees. There are 10 vertices, and each has a degree of 6.

Sum of Degrees = 10 x 6 = 60.

Step 2: Solve for the number of edges.

60 = 2 x (Number of Edges)

Number of Edges = 60 / 2 = 30.

Subjective Question 2

(2) In a football competition with 12 teams, each team plays every other team once. How many matches will take place?

Solving with Graph Theory

This can be represented as a complete graph with 12 vertices. The number of matches is the number of edges in this complete graph.

Method 1: Formula for edges in a complete graph.

Number of Edges = n(n-1) / 2

Where 'n' is the number of vertices (teams).

Number of Matches = 12 x (12 - 1) / 2 = 12 x 11 / 2 = 132 / 2 = 66.

Method 2: Sum of Degrees. In a complete graph of 12 vertices, each vertex has a degree of (n-1) = 11. Sum of Degrees = 12 x 11 = 132. Number of Edges = Sum of Degrees / 2 = 132 / 2 = 66.

Subjective Question 3

(3) Draw the directed graph D with V = {V₁, V₂, V₃, V₄, V₅, V₆} and E = {(V₁,V₃), (V₂,V₃), (V₃,V₄), (V₄,V₁), (V₄,V₅), (V₅,V₆)}.

Step 3: Draw an edge from V₂ to V₃ and add an arrow pointing to V₃.

Subjective Question 4

(4) Flight times: BJ-KL (6h), BJ-BK (4h), KL-S (1h), KL-BK (3h), S-BK (4h). (a) Draw the graph. (b) Find the minimum time from Singapore (S) to Beijing (BJ).

(a) Draw the Weighted Graph

Similar to Q10, draw the 4 vertices (BJ, KL, BK, S) and connect them with edges, writing the flight time (weight) next to each edge.

(b) Find Minimum Time

Step 1: Identify possible routes from S to BJ.

Route 1: S → KL → BJ. Total time = Time(S→KL) + Time(KL→BJ) = 1 + 6 = 7 hours.

Route 2: S → BK → BJ. Total time = Time(S→BK) + Time(BK→BJ) = 4 + 4 = 8 hours.

Route 3: S → KL → BK → BJ. Total time = 1 + 3 + 4 = 8 hours.

Step 2: Compare the times. The minimum time is the smallest total from the possible routes.

Minimum time = 7 hours.

Subjective Question 5 & 6

(5) Represent the security groups as a graph. Determine if the schedule is fair.

(6) Represent the chemical storage restrictions as a graph.

Guidance for Q5 and Q6

These questions involve modeling real-world scenarios with graphs.

For Q5: One way is to create a bipartite graph. One set of vertices for the officers (Amir, Budi, etc.) and another set for the groups (K1, K2, etc.). Draw an edge from an officer to a group if they are in that group. To check if it's "fair," count the degree of each officer's vertex. The degree represents how many groups they are in. If the degrees are very different, the schedule is not fair.

For Q6: Draw 8 vertices for the chemicals (A-H). Draw an edge between two chemicals if they *cannot* be stored together. For example, since A cannot be stored with C, E, and H, you will draw edges (A,C), (A,E), and (A,H).
