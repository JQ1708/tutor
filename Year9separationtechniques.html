Distillation

Simple Distillation

What is Simple Distillation for?

Tap to find out!

Use of Simple Distillation

It's used to separate a liquid and a soluble solid from a solution.

Example: Separating pure water from a solution of salt water.

The Process: Part 1

Heating & Evaporation

Step 1: Evaporation

  • The solution is heated in a flask.
  • The pure water evaporates, producing a vapour.
  • This vapour rises through the neck of the flask.

(Remember to refer to the diagram of the simple distillation apparatus!)

The Process: Part 2

Cooling & Condensing

Step 2: Condensation

  • The vapour passes through the condenser.
  • Inside the condenser, it cools down and condenses.
  • Condensing means it turns back into a pure liquid.
  • The pure liquid is then collected in a beaker.

What's Left Behind?

The Solute

After all the water has evaporated and been collected, only the solid solute (e.g., salt) will be left behind in the original flask.

Examiner's Top Tip!

Condenser Water Flow

Condenser Direction

If you're asked to draw or label the apparatus, remember this rule:

  • Cooling water goes IN at the BOTTOM (near the beaker).
  • Cooling water comes OUT at the TOP (near the flask).

    • This ensures the entire jacket is always full of cold water for efficient cooling!

Fractional Distillation

What is Fractional Distillation for?

Tap to find out!

Use of Fractional Distillation

It's used to separate two or more miscible liquids (liquids that mix together).

The liquids must have different boiling points.

Example: Separating ethanol and water from a mixture of the two.

The Process: Step 1

Heating to the Right Temp

Heating the Mixture

  • The solution is heated to the temperature of the substance with the lowest boiling point.
  • This substance will evaporate first and turn into a vapour.

(Remember to refer to the diagram of the fractional distillation apparatus!)

The Magic Component!

The Fractionating Column

The Fractionating Column

  • The vapour rises through a special piece of equipment called a fractionating column.
  • This column is filled with glass beads or rods.
  • This provides a large surface area for evaporation and condensation to happen over and over again.

Separation in Action

Reaching the Top

Collecting the First Liquid

  • When the vapour of the substance with the lower boiling point reaches the top of the column, it passes into the condenser.
  • It then cools, condenses, and is collected.
  • The liquid with the higher boiling point will also evaporate, but it will cool and condense on the glass beads and fall back down into the flask.

Filtration

What is Filtration for?

Tap to find out!

Use of Filtration

Used to separate an undissolved (insoluble) solid from a liquid or solution.

Example: Separating sand from water.

(Remember to refer to the diagram of the filtration apparatus!)

The Apparatus

What do you need?

Equipment for Filtration

You use filter paper placed inside a filter funnel.

  • The filter paper has tiny pores that are large enough for water molecules to pass through, but too small for solid particles.

The Results

Residue & Filtrate

What You Get

  • Residue: The insoluble solid that gets trapped in the filter paper.
  • Filtrate: The liquid or solution that passes through the filter paper and is collected.

Crystallisation

What is Crystallisation for?

Tap to find out!

Use of Crystallisation

Used to separate a dissolved solid from a solution, when the solid is much more soluble in a hot solvent than a cold one.

Example: Getting pure copper sulfate crystals from a solution.

The Process: Step 1

Heating the Solution

Creating a Saturated Solution

  • The solution is heated gently to evaporate some of the solvent (e.g., water).
  • This makes the solution more concentrated.
  • You stop heating when it reaches the 'point of crystallisation' - when crystals form on a cold glass rod dipped into the solution.

The Process: Step 2

Cooling Down

Forming the Crystals

  • The saturated solution is left to cool down slowly.
  • As it cools, the solubility of the solid decreases, and it forms crystals.

The Process: Step 3

Collecting & Finishing

Getting Pure Crystals

  • The crystals are collected by filtering them from the remaining solution.
  • They are then washed with a small amount of cold distilled water (to remove impurities).
  • Finally, they are dried (e.g., by pressing between filter papers or in a warm oven).

Chromatography

What is Chromatography for?

Tap to find out!

Use of Chromatography

Used to separate small amounts of substances from a mixture.

Example: Separating the different coloured dyes in an ink or food colouring.

Key Concepts

The Two Phases

Mobile & Stationary Phases

  • Stationary Phase: The substance that does not move. In paper chromatography, this is the paper itself.
  • Mobile Phase: The substance that moves. This is the solvent (e.g., water or ethanol) that moves up the paper.

How Does It Work?

The Principle of Separation

Separation depends on how the substances in the mixture interact with the two phases.

  • A substance that is more soluble in the mobile phase and has less attraction to the stationary phase will travel further up the paper.

The Method: Step 1

Setting Up

Preparation

(Refer to the diagram of the chromatography setup!)

  1. Draw a straight line in pencil about 1.5 cm from the bottom of the chromatography paper. This is the origin line.
  2. Use a capillary tube to put a tiny spot of the sample onto the origin line.
  3. Place the paper in a beaker with a small amount of solvent, ensuring the solvent level is BELOW the pencil line.

Crucial Practical Tip!

The Origin Line

The pencil origin line must never be below the level of the solvent.

Why? Because if it is, the samples will just wash away into the solvent instead of travelling up the paper!

The Method: Step 2

Running & Finishing

Completing the Chromatogram

  1. Allow the solvent to travel undisturbed at least three-quarters of the way up the paper.
  2. Remove the paper and immediately draw another pencil line on the wet edge. This is the solvent front.
  3. Let the paper dry. The finished result is called a chromatogram.

Calculating Rf Values

The Rf Formula

The Rf (retardation factor) value is a ratio. It is always less than 1.

Rf   =  
Distance moved by the spot
Distance moved by the solvent

Both distances are measured in mm from the pencil origin line.

Using Rf Values

How do they help?

Identifying Substances

Under the same conditions (same paper, same solvent, same temperature), a particular substance will always have the same Rf value.

You can compare the Rf value of an unknown substance to the Rf values of known substances to identify it.
