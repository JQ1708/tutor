1. The Anatomy of an Atom

Every element is made of tiny particles called atoms. They are the fundamental building blocks of matter. While incredibly small, they are composed of even smaller subatomic particles: protons, neutrons, and electrons. The central part, the nucleus, contains the protons and neutrons, while the electrons orbit this core in distinct energy levels or shells.

2. Identifying Elements

Atomic Number (Z)

This is the number of protons in the nucleus. It's the unique ID for each element. An atom's identity is defined solely by its atomic number.

6 → Carbon

Mass Number (A)

This is the total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus. To find the number of neutrons, simply subtract the atomic number from the mass number.

Neutrons = A - Z

3. Electron Shells

Electrons are not randomly placed; they occupy specific energy levels called shells. These shells fill up from the inside out, and the arrangement of electrons, known as the electronic structure, dictates how an atom behaves chemically.

4. Group 1: The Alkali Metals

Reaction with Water

Alkali metals are extremely reactive, especially with water. Their reactivity increases as you go down the group. Click the buttons to see what happens!

Why the Trend?

All Group 1 elements have one electron in their outermost shell. They are desperate to lose this electron to achieve a stable, full inner shell.

As you go down the group, the atoms get larger. The outermost electron is further from the positive nucleus and is shielded by more inner shells. This makes it easier to lose, hence the increased reactivity.

5. Ionic Bonding: The Transfer

Metal Meets Non-Metal

When a reactive metal like Sodium (Na) meets a reactive non-metal like Chlorine (Cl), the metal doesn't just lose its electron—it transfers it to the non-metal.

This creates a positive ion (Na⁺) and a negative ion (Cl⁻). The powerful electrostatic attraction between these opposite charges forms a strong ionic bond.

Na (2,8,1) + Cl (2,8,7) → Na⁺[2,8] Cl⁻[2,8,8]

6. Covalent Bonding: The Partnership

O (2,6) + 2H (1) → H₂O

Sharing is Caring

When two non-metals bond, they don't transfer electrons. Instead, they share them. Each atom contributes electrons to form shared pairs, creating a strong covalent bond that holds them together in a discrete unit called a molecule.

In water (H₂O), oxygen shares electrons with two hydrogen atoms so that all three atoms achieve a stable, full outer shell.
