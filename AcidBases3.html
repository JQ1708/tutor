stage 2 Sulfur dioxide is converted into sulfur trioxide.

(i) How is sulfur converted into sulfur dioxide in stage 1?

Sulfur is burned or heated in air (or oxygen).

(ii) Describe how sulfur dioxide is converted into sulfur trioxide in stage 2. Your answer should include: an equation for the reaction, the temperature used, the name of the catalyst used.

(iii) The reaction in stage 2 can reach equilibrium. What is meant by the term equilibrium?

Tap to reveal answer

Equilibrium is a state in a reversible reaction where the rate of the forward reaction and the rate of the backward reaction are equal, and the concentrations of reactants and products are constant.

Question 18

(0620/41_Summer_2020_Q4) - In The Lab (chemical Test& Salt Analysis), Acids And Bases

This question is about reactions of bases and acids.

(a) Ammonia is a gas at room temperature. What is the test for ammonia gas? Describe the positive result of this test.

Tap to reveal answer

Test: Place a piece of damp red litmus paper at the mouth of the test tube.

Result: The litmus paper turns blue.

(b) Ammonia reacts with water to form ions. NH₃ + H₂O ⇌ NH₄⁺ + OH⁻

(i) How does this equation show that ammonia, NH₃, behaves as a base?

Tap to reveal answer

It acts as a proton acceptor, taking a proton (H⁺) from a water molecule.

(ii) Aqueous ammonia is described as a weak base. Suggest the pH of aqueous ammonia.

Tap to reveal answer

Any pH value above 7 and up to 11 would be a reasonable suggestion (e.g., pH 9).

(iii) Describe what is seen when aqueous ammonia is added to aqueous copper(II) sulfate, until no further change is seen.

Tap to reveal answer

Initially, a light blue precipitate of copper(II) hydroxide is formed. As more aqueous ammonia is added, the precipitate dissolves to form a deep blue solution.

(c) Aqueous sodium hydroxide, NaOH(aq), is a strong alkali that reacts with dilute sulfuric acid exothermically.

(i) What type of reaction is this?

Tap to reveal answer

Neutralisation

(ii) Complete the equation for the reaction between aqueous sodium hydroxide and dilute sulfuric acid.

2NaOH + H₂SO₄ →

Tap to reveal answer

2NaOH + H₂SO₄ → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O

(d) A student wanted to find the concentration of some dilute sulfuric acid by titration. The student found that 25.0 cm³ of 0.0400 mol/dm³ NaOH(aq) reacted exactly with 20.0 cm³ of H₂SO₄(aq).

(i) Name a suitable indicator to use in this titration.

Tap to reveal answer

Methyl orange or phenolphthalein.

(ii) Calculate the concentration of the H₂SO₄(aq) in mol/dm³ using the following steps.

Tap to reveal answer

Moles of NaOH = concentration x volume = 0.0400 x (25.0 / 1000) = 0.00100 mol.

From the equation 2NaOH + H₂SO₄, the mole ratio is 2:1. Moles of H₂SO₄ = 0.00100 / 2 = 0.000500 mol.

Concentration of H₂SO₄ = moles / volume = 0.000500 / (20.0 / 1000) = 0.025 mol/dm³.

(iii) Calculate the concentration of the 0.0400 mol/dm³ NaOH(aq) in g/dm³.

Tap to reveal answer

Mᵣ of NaOH = 23 + 16 + 1 = 40.

Concentration in g/dm³ = concentration in mol/dm³ x Mᵣ = 0.0400 x 40 = 1.6 g/dm³.

Question 20

(0620/42_Winter_2020_Q2) - Acids And Bases, Using Moles, Electricity And Chemical Changes

Soluble salts can be made by adding a metal carbonate to a dilute acid.

(a) Give the formula of the dilute acid which reacts with a metal carbonate to form a nitrate salt.

Tap to reveal answer

HNO₃

(b) A student wanted to make hydrated iron(II) sulfate crystals, FeSO₄.xH₂O, by adding excess iron(II) carbonate to dilute sulfuric acid.

(i) Why must the iron(II) carbonate be added in excess in step 2?

Tap to reveal answer

To make sure all the sulfuric acid reacts so the final salt is not contaminated with acid.

(ii) State two observations in step 2 that would show that iron(II) carbonate was in excess.

Tap to reveal answer

1. There is no more fizzing (effervescence).

2. The iron(II) carbonate stops dissolving and an unreacted solid remains.

(iii) Describe what should be done during step 3 to ensure there is a maximum yield of crystals.

Tap to reveal answer

You should rinse the residue (with a small amount of distilled water) to wash any remaining dissolved salt into the filtrate.

(iv) A saturated solution is formed in step 4. Describe what a saturated solution is.

Tap to reveal answer

A saturated solution is a solution that can dissolve no more solute at a specified temperature.

(v) Name a different compound that could be used instead of iron(II) carbonate to produce hydrated iron(II) sulfate crystals from dilute sulfuric acid.

Tap to reveal answer

iron(II) oxide or iron(II) hydroxide.

(c) On analysing the crystals, the student found that one mole of the hydrated iron(II) sulfate crystals, FeSO₄.xH₂O, had a mass of 278 g. Determine the value of x.

Tap to reveal answer

Mass of 1 mole of FeSO₄ = 56 + 32 + (16 x 4) = 152 g.

Mass of water in 1 mole of crystals = 278 - 152 = 126 g.

Moles of water = mass of water / Mᵣ of water = 126 / 18 = 7. So, x = 7.

(d) An insoluble salt, silver bromide, is made.

(i) State the term used to describe this method of making salts.

Tap to reveal answer

Precipitation

(ii) Give the observation the student would make during step 1.

Tap to reveal answer

A cream precipitate would form.

(iii) Write the ionic equation for the reaction between aqueous silver nitrate and aqueous potassium bromide. Include state symbols.

Tap to reveal answer

Ag⁺(aq) + Br⁻(aq) → AgBr(s)

(e) Calculate the volume of chlorine gas, in cm³, that reacts to form 2.34g of NaCl. 2Na(s) + Cl₂(g) → 2NaCl(s)

Tap to reveal answer

Mᵣ NaCl = 23 + 35.5 = 58.5. Moles of NaCl = 2.34 / 58.5 = 0.0400 mol.

Mole ratio Cl₂ : NaCl is 1:2. Moles of Cl₂ = 0.0400 / 2 = 0.0200 mol.

Volume of Cl₂ = moles x 24000 = 0.0200 x 24000 = 480 cm³.

(f) Sodium chloride does not conduct electricity when solid, but does conduct electricity when molten.

(i) Explain why, in terms of structure and bonding.

Tap to reveal answer

Solid sodium chloride has ions that are held in a fixed lattice structure and are not free to move. When molten, the ions are mobile and can move to carry charge.

(ii) Name the product formed at the positive electrode when electricity is passed through molten sodium chloride.

Tap to reveal answer

Chlorine

(iii) State the type of change that occurs at the positive electrode in (ii). Explain your answer in terms of electron transfer.

Tap to reveal answer

Type of change: Oxidation

Explanation: Electrons are lost by the chloride ions.

(iv) Describe what else can be done to sodium chloride to allow it to conduct electricity.

Tap to reveal answer

Dissolve it in water.

(d) When hydrated magnesium sulfate crystals, MgSO₄·xH₂O, are heated they give off water.

This question is about salts.

(a) Describe how you would make a pure sample of lead(II) iodide by precipitation.

Tap to reveal answer

First, dissolve solid lead(II) nitrate and solid sodium iodide in water to make two separate solutions. Mix the two solutions together to form a yellow precipitate of lead(II) iodide. Filter the mixture to separate the solid. Wash the residue with distilled water and then dry it. The equation is Pb(NO₃)₂ + 2NaI → 2NaNO₃ + PbI₂.

(b) Nitrates decompose when heated.

(i) When hydrated zinc nitrate is heated, oxygen gas is given off. Describe a test for oxygen.

Tap to reveal answer

Test: Insert a glowing splint into the test tube containing the gas.

Observation: The splint relights.

(ii) Complete the equation for the decomposition of hydrated zinc nitrate.

2Zn(NO₃)₂.6H₂O → ............ + ............NO₂ + O₂ + .....H₂O

Tap to reveal answer

2Zn(NO₃)₂.6H₂O → 2ZnO + 4NO₂ + O₂ + 12H₂O

(c) Some sulfates are hydrated.

(i) Describe how the student can check that all the water has been given off.

Tap to reveal answer

The student should heat the solid and weigh it again, and repeat this process until the mass is constant.

(ii) In an experiment, 1.61 g of Na₂SO₄·xH₂O is heated until all the water is given off. The mass of Na₂SO₄ remaining is 0.71g. Determine the value of x.

Tap to reveal answer

Moles of Na₂SO₄ = 0.71 / 142 = 0.005 mol.

Mass of water = 1.61 - 0.71 = 0.9 g.

Moles of water = 0.9 / 18 = 0.05 mol.

Ratio of moles Na₂SO₄ : H₂O = 0.005 : 0.05 = 1 : 10. So, x = 10.

Question 24

(0620/43_Summer_2021_Q5) - Acids And Bases, Reacting Masses And Chemical Equations

This question is about salts.

(a) Describe how you would make a pure sample of zinc sulfate crystals.

Tap to reveal answer

Add zinc carbonate to sulfuric acid until it stops dissolving or there is no more effervescence, indicating the acid is used up. Filter the mixture to remove the excess zinc carbonate. Heat the filtrate until saturated, then allow it to cool and crystallise. The equation is ZnCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + CO₂ + H₂O.

(b) Some sulfates decompose when heated.

(i) Describe a test for sulfur dioxide.

Tap to reveal answer

Test: Bubble the gas through acidified aqueous potassium manganate(VII).

Observation: The solution turns from purple to colourless.

(ii) Complete the equation for the decomposition of hydrated iron(II) sulfate.

.....FeSO₄·7H₂O → Fe₂O₃ + SO₂ + SO₃ + .....H₂O

Tap to reveal answer

2FeSO₄·7H₂O → Fe₂O₃ + SO₂ + SO₃ + 14H₂O

(c) Some chlorides are hydrated.

(i) Describe how the student can be sure that all the water is given off.

Tap to reveal answer

The student should heat the solid and weigh it, then repeat this process until the mass is constant.

(ii) In an experiment, 4.88 g of BaCl₂·xH₂O is heated until all the water is given off. The mass of BaCl₂ remaining is 4.16 g. Determine the value of x.

Tap to reveal answer

Moles of BaCl₂ = 4.16 / 208 = 0.02 mol.

Mass of water = 4.88 - 4.16 = 0.72 g.

Moles of water = 0.72 / 18 = 0.04 mol.

Ratio of moles BaCl₂ : H₂O = 0.02 : 0.04 = 1 : 2. So, x = 2.
